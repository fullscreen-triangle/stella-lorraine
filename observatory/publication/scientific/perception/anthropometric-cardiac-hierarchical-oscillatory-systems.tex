\subsection{The Multi-Scale Coordination Problem}

Biological organisms maintain coordinated function across temporal scales spanning fourteen orders of magnitude, from molecular vibrations at femtosecond timescales ($10^{-15}$ s) to circadian rhythms at kilosecond scales ($10^{4}$ s) \citep{glass2001synchronization,strogatz2003sync}. Traditional approaches treat oscillatory systems independently: neuroscience examines cortical rhythms \citep{fries2005mechanism}, cardiology studies heart rate variability \citep{malik1996heart}, and biomechanics analyzes gait dynamics \citep{hausdorff2007gait}. This domain-specific fragmentation prevents comprehensive understanding of biological temporal organization. \section{Mathematical Foundations}

\subsection{General Oscillatory Systems Theory}

We define biological oscillatory systems following established dynamical systems formalism \citep{strogatz2014nonlinear}:

\begin{definition}[Biological Oscillator]
A biological oscillatory system is characterized by the differential equation
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\mu}, t)
\label{eq:general_oscillator}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ represents the system state, $\boldsymbol{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^p$ denotes system parameters, and $\mathbf{f}: \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^p \times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^n$ describes the dynamics.
\end{definition}

An oscillatory solution satisfies the periodicity condition:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x}(t + T) = \mathbf{x}(t)
\label{eq:periodicity}
\end{equation}
for period $T > 0$. The fundamental frequency is $\omega_0 = 2\pi/T$.

\subsection{Phase Dynamics and Synchronization}

Phase provides coordinate-independent description of oscillatory state \citep{pikovsky2001synchronization}. For oscillator $i$, the phase $\theta_i(t) \in [0, 2\pi)$ evolves according to:
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\theta_i}{dt} = \omega_i + \sum_{j} K_{ij} H(\theta_j - \theta_i)
\label{eq:phase_dynamics}
\end{equation}
where $\omega_i$ is the natural frequency, $K_{ij}$ represents coupling strength, and $H$ is the coupling function.

For weak sinusoidal coupling, the Kuramoto model applies \citep{kuramoto1984chemical}:
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\theta_i}{dt} = \omega_i + \frac{K}{N}\sum_{j=1}^N \sin(\theta_j - \theta_i)
\label{eq:kuramoto}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Phase-Locking Value}

Phase-locking between two oscillators is quantified through the Phase-Locking Value \citep{lachaux1999measuring}:

\begin{definition}[Phase-Locking Value]
For two oscillatory signals $x_1(t)$ and $x_2(t)$ with instantaneous phases $\theta_1(t)$ and $\theta_2(t)$, the PLV is defined as
\begin{equation}
\text{PLV}_{12} = \left|\left\langle e^{i(\theta_1(t) - \theta_2(t))}\right\rangle_t\right|
\label{eq:plv}
\end{equation}
where $\langle \cdot \rangle_t$ denotes temporal average and $| \cdot |$ denotes complex magnitude.
\end{definition}

The PLV ranges from 0 (no phase relationship) to 1 (perfect phase-locking). Values exceeding 0.7 indicate strong synchronization \citep{mormann2000mean}.

\subsection{Hierarchical Oscillatory Systems}

Biological oscillatory hierarchies exhibit multi-scale structure \citep{buzsaki2006rhythms}. We formalize this through scale separation:

\begin{definition}[Two-Scale Oscillatory System]
A system with fast variable $u$ and slow variable $v$ satisfies
\begin{align}
\frac{du}{dt} &= \frac{1}{\epsilon}F(u,v) \label{eq:fast_scale}\\
\frac{dv}{dt} &= G(u,v) \label{eq:slow_scale}
\end{align}
where $0 < \epsilon \ll 1$ represents the scale separation parameter.
\end{definition}

The averaging method provides reduced dynamics \citep{sanders2007averaging}:
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\bar{v}}{dt} = \frac{1}{T_0}\int_0^{T_0} G(u_0(t;\bar{v}), \bar{v}) \, dt
\label{eq:averaged_dynamics}
\end{equation}
where $u_0(t;\bar{v})$ is the periodic solution of the fast subsystem for fixed $\bar{v}$.

\subsection{Stability Analysis via Floquet Theory}

Stability of periodic orbits is determined through monodromy matrix analysis \citep{chicone2006ordinary}:

\begin{theorem}[Floquet Stability]
The periodic orbit $\mathbf{x}_0(t)$ with period $T$ is asymptotically stable if all characteristic multipliers $\lambda_i$ of the monodromy matrix $\mathbf{M} = \boldsymbol{\Phi}(T)$ satisfy $|\lambda_i| < 1$ for $i \neq 1$, where $\boldsymbol{\Phi}(t)$ solves
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\boldsymbol{\Phi}}{dt} = \mathbf{J}(\mathbf{x}_0(t))\boldsymbol{\Phi}, \quad \boldsymbol{\Phi}(0) = \mathbf{I}
\end{equation}
and $\mathbf{J}$ is the Jacobian matrix $J_{ij} = \partial f_i/\partial x_j$.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Frequency Ratio Quantization}

Phase-locked oscillators exhibit frequency ratios approximating rational numbers \citep{glass2001synchronization}:

\begin{theorem}[Rational Frequency Locking]
For two coupled oscillators with natural frequencies $\omega_1$ and $\omega_2$, phase-locking occurs at frequency ratio
\begin{equation}
\frac{\omega_1}{\omega_2} = \frac{m}{n}
\end{equation}
where $m, n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$ are relatively prime integers, provided coupling strength exceeds threshold $K > K_c(m,n)$.
\end{theorem}

Small integer ratios ($m,n \leq 5$) exhibit widest locking ranges (Arnold tongues) in parameter space \citep{jensen1984complete}.

\section{Visualization Methodology}

\subsection{Horizon Chart Adaptation for Biological Data}

Display of twelve simultaneous oscillatory signals presents substantial visualization challenges. Traditional methods suffer critical limitations:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Vertical stacking}: Requires $n \times h$ pixels for $n$ signals at height $h$, consuming excessive display space.
\item \textbf{Overlaying}: Produces occlusion and visual confusion, obscuring phase relationships.
\item \textbf{Small multiples}: Requires large horizontal separation preventing phase comparison.
\end{itemize}

We adapt horizon chart methodology \citep{heer2009sizing,saito2005two} to biological oscillatory data through cardiac phase normalization.

\subsection{Horizon Chart Construction}

Standard horizon charts achieve spatial compression through three operations:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Mirroring}: Negative values reflected around zero baseline and colored red; positive values remain blue.
\item \textbf{Banding}: Signal divided into uniform value bands (typically 2--4 bands).
\item \textbf{Layering}: Bands stacked vertically with color intensity encoding band level.
\end{enumerate}

This achieves $2 \times n_{\text{bands}}$ vertical compression. For $n_{\text{bands}} = 3$, compression factor is $6\times$.

Heer et al. \citep{heer2009sizing} established optimal chart heights through psychophysical experiments:
\begin{itemize}
\item 2-band: 12 pixels minimum (3.4 mm at 96 DPI)
\item 3-band: 18 pixels minimum (5.1 mm at 96 DPI)
\item 4-band: 24 pixels minimum (6.8 mm at 96 DPI)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cardiac-Referenced Extension}

We extend horizon charts through cardiac phase normalization:

\textbf{Phase Normalization}: For each oscillatory signal $x_i(t)$, we normalize time to cardiac phase:
\begin{equation}
\phi_{\text{cardiac}}(t) = 2\pi \frac{t - t_{R,k}}{t_{R,k+1} - t_{R,k}}
\end{equation}
where $t_{R,k}$ and $t_{R,k+1}$ are successive R-wave times satisfying $t_{R,k} \leq t < t_{R,k+1}$.

Signals are resampled: $x_i(t) \rightarrow x_i(\phi_{\text{cardiac}})$, aligning all oscillations to cardiac cycle.

\textbf{Standardization}: Each signal is standardized across cardiac cycles:
\begin{equation}
z_i(\phi) = \frac{x_i(\phi) - \mu_i(\phi)}{\sigma_i(\phi)}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_i(\phi)$ and $\sigma_i(\phi)$ are mean and standard deviation at phase $\phi$ computed across all cardiac cycles.

\textbf{Band Definition}: Standardized signals are divided into bands:
\begin{equation}
B_k = \{z : (k-1)\sigma < z \leq k\sigma\}, \quad k \in \{-3, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, 3\}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Color Encoding}:
\begin{itemize}
\item Positive deviations ($z > 0$): Blue, intensity $\propto$ band level
\item Negative deviations ($z < 0$): Red, intensity $\propto$ band level
\item Cardiac reference: Yellow (neutral, high visibility)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Hierarchical Arrangement}: Signals arranged vertically by frequency (fastest to slowest), with cardiac reference highlighted centrally.

\subsection{Phase-Locking Visualization}

Horizon charts reveal phase-locking through visual patterns:

\textbf{Perfect phase-locking} (PLV = 1): Signal peaks align with constant cardiac phase, producing vertical stripe patterns in horizon bands.

\textbf{Partial phase-locking} (0.5 $<$ PLV $<$ 0.9): Signal peaks exhibit phase drift relative to cardiac rhythm, producing diagonal band patterns. Drift rate inversely proportional to PLV.

\textbf{No phase-locking} (PLV $<$ 0.3): Signal peaks randomly distributed across cardiac phases, producing irregular, noise-like patterns.

This enables immediate visual assessment of phase-locking quality across all oscillatory scales simultaneously—information inaccessible in traditional displays.
